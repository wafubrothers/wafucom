Q1.

Why are the leak tester results inconsistent each time?

1. Possible Causes

1. Issues with the tester itself

  • Sensor precision degradation: Aging pressure or flow sensors can cause data fluctuations
  • Air source pressure instability: Inconsistent pressure from pumps or sources affects inflation/vacuum accuracy
  • Inconsistent valve response: Variance in solenoid valve switching time leads to pressure control deviation

2. Testing condition changes

  • Environmental fluctuations: Temperature variations affect gas volume and pressure readings
  • Fixture seal failure: Gaps between fixture and workpiece may cause false leaks
  • Workpiece interference: Material deformation or residual liquid causes temporary false positives

3. Human operational factors

  • Insufficient or inconsistent fixture clamping force
  • Non-standardized testing procedure (e.g., varying inflation time)
  • Testing started before system stabilization

2. Solutions

1. Device optimization

  • Regular calibration and maintenance: Calibrate sensors periodically and replace aging components
  • Hardware upgrades: Use higher-precision sensors and faster-responding valves
  • Improve air supply stability: Add regulators or air tanks to ensure consistent supply pressure

2. Controlled testing environment

  • Constant temperature and humidity: Keep testing area in a controlled environment
  • Pre-process workpiece: Clean surface and follow standard clamping procedures
  • Software compensation: Use algorithms to compensate for temperature/humidity effects

3. Standardized operation process

  • Standard operation manual: Define inflation time, stabilization time, etc.
  • Automation over manual operation: Use robots for consistent clamping and testing
  • Operator training: Ensure strict adherence to operation guidelines
Q2.

Why does the accuracy drop when the leak tester operates above 500kPa?

1. Possible Causes

1. Nonlinear sensor response

  • Sensor range limitations: Pressure sensor may exceed optimal linear range at high pressures
  • Temperature drift: Sensor heat buildup at high pressure can cause zero-point shift if not temperature-compensated

2. Sealing and leakage issues

  • Seal material deformation: High pressure may over-compress O-rings, causing microleaks
  • Structural deformation: Chamber or piping may elastically deform under high pressure

3. Air supply stability and response speed

  • Insufficient air pump capacity: Maintaining stable high pressure may be difficult if pump power is lacking
  • Valve response delay: Higher resistance under high pressure reduces valve control accuracy

4. Changes in gas physical properties

  • Gas compressibility effects: Gases deviate from ideal behavior under high pressure
  • Adiabatic effect: Rapid inflation raises gas temperature, affecting pressure decay curve

2. Solutions

1. Hardware optimization

  • Use high-precision sensors: Select sensors with wide range and minimal nonlinearity
  • Reinforce sealing design: Use high-pressure-rated seals or metal sealing structures
  • Anti-deformation structural design: Use thick-walled metal chambers and validate deformation range

2. Gas circuit and control system improvement

  • Dynamic temperature compensation: Real-time temperature monitoring to adjust gas equations
  • Optimized pressure decay model: Use nonlinear leakage models instead of linear approximations
  • High-pressure air pump: Choose more stable air supply systems designed for high pressure

3. Standardized testing procedures

  • Extend stabilization time: Define and enforce thermal balance duration at high pressure
  • Segmented testing strategy: Use different parameters for low- and high-pressure stages
  • Pre-pressurization step: Reduce thermal drift through pre-pressurization
Q3.

What is the working principle of an airtightness tester?

Differential Pressure Method

Compares a good part with a defective part to determine sealing integrity by measuring the pressure difference between a standard and the tested part.

Flow Method

Determines sealing integrity by measuring the flow rate of leaked gas, monitoring the volume of gas flow over time to identify leakage.

Direct Pressure Method

Detects leaks by observing changes in internal gas pressure, either by blowing air in or drawing it out, and checking pressure variations.

Q1.

What should I do if the test result is negative?

Possible Causes:

1. Temperature changes

Temperature changes in the environment or the product under test can cause gas expansion or contraction, affecting pressure readings and possibly resulting in negative leakage values. Especially after rapid inflation, the gas heats up and cools down afterward, causing pressure to drop.

2. Product or fixture deformation

The product may deform under pressure, changing the internal volume and resulting in pressure changes. Likewise, minor deformations in the fixture may affect test results.

Solutions:

1. Temperature Control

  • Stable environment: Keep testing environment temperature stable to avoid pressure fluctuations
  • Pre-heat/cool workpieces: Ensure the tested part is at the same temperature as the environment
  • Increase balance time: Allow time for temperature stabilization after inflation
  • Temperature compensation: Use devices or algorithms that support temperature compensation

2. Equipment Inspection

  • Fixture inspection: Regularly inspect fixtures and pipelines for deformation or damage
  • Seal replacement: Check and replace aged or damaged seals
  • Stable volume: Ensure consistent test volume to avoid pressure changes from loose fixtures
  • Device calibration: Fully calibrate equipment to ensure sensor accuracy
Q2.

Why are the test results inconsistent?

Possible Causes:

1. Inconsistent operation

Different handling methods, clamping force, inflation time, and balance time may cause result variation.

2. Environmental factors

Temperature and humidity fluctuations can affect result stability, especially in uncontrolled environments.

3. Equipment calibration issues

Lack of regular calibration or improper calibration can lead to sensor aging or drift, reducing consistency.

Solutions:

1. Standardize operation

  • Create SOPs: Define detailed procedures and parameters
  • Operator training: Provide unified training and assessment
  • Use automation: Apply fixtures or automation for consistency

2. Control environment

  • Control temperature/humidity: Conduct testing in a stable environment
  • Avoid airflow: Stay away from air vents or doors/windows
  • Monitor conditions: Record and monitor environmental data

3. Regular calibration

  • Calibration schedule: Follow the recommended frequency
  • Professional calibration: Use standard tools and qualified personnel
  • Record tracking: Keep logs to track performance changes
Q3.

How to troubleshoot a leak tester malfunction?

If the leak tester malfunctions, follow these steps to identify and resolve the issue:

1

Check power and connections

Ensure the device is properly powered, the power cable is intact, and all connections to air source or computer are secure.

2

Check error codes

Refer to the error code list in the user manual to understand the cause and solution of the error shown on the display.

3

Clean and maintain

Regularly clean the air system, sensors, and seals to remove dust or debris. Check for aged or damaged seals and replace if necessary.

4

Restart the device

Try turning off the power, wait a few minutes, then restart the device. A simple reboot may solve temporary issues.

5

Contact technical support

If the issue persists, contact our technical support team with detailed fault description and device information for further assistance.
